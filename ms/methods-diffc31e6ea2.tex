\hypertarget{model}{%
\section{Model}\label{model}}

We modeled the seedling survival for the dry and rainy seasons
separately. Since the effect of tree neighbors on seedling survival is
nonlinear on a logistic scale (\protect\hyperlink{ref-Detto2019}{Detto
\emph{et al.} 2019}), we performed a grid-search for the scaling
parameter \emph{c} between 0 and 1 in 0.01 increments that maximized the
likelihood of the following survival model,

\[
\mathrm{logit}(p_i) = b_0 + b_1 Z_{1i}^c + b_2 Z_{2i}^c,
\]

where \(p_i\) is the individual survival probability in the \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{ith }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \emph{\DIFadd{i}}\DIFadd{th
}\DIFaddend census interval, and \(Z_1\) and \(Z_2\) are distance-weighted sums of
basal areas of conspecifics and heterospecifics respectively. We found
that \(c\) = \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{0.24 }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{0.27 }\DIFaddend for the dry season and \(c\) = \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{0.27 }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{0.24 }\DIFaddend for the rainy
season were the best estimates for our dataset.

We built Bayesian hierarchical models that include variation among
species in the effects of conspecific and heterospecific neighbours, and
rainfall on survival. Survival (\(s\)) of seedling record \emph{i} of
individual \emph{m} for species \emph{j} in census \emph{t} in plot
\emph{p} was modeled using the Bernoulli distribution (\(\mathcal{B}\)):

\[
s_{i,j,m,t,p} \sim \mathcal{B}(p_{i, j, m, t, p}),
\]

\[
\mathrm{logit}(p_{i,j,m,t,p}) = \DIFdelbegin %DIFDELCMD < \boldsymbol{\beta_{j}} %%%
\DIFdel{\cdot }\DIFdelend \boldsymbol{x_{i}} \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{\cdot }\boldsymbol{\beta_{j}} \DIFaddend + \phi_p + \omega_t + \psi_m,
\]

where
\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{\(\boldsymbol{\beta_{j}} = \left[\beta_{j,1}, \beta_{j,2}, \ldots, \beta_{j,K} \right]\)
}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{\(\boldsymbol{\beta_{j}} = \left[\beta_{1,j}, \beta_{2,j}, \ldots, \beta_{K,j} \right]\)
}\DIFaddend is the coefficient \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{row }\DIFaddend \emph{K}-vector for species \emph{j}, \emph{K} is
the number of predictors for an individual seedling,
\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{\(\boldsymbol{x_i} = \left[x_{1,i},x _{2,i}, \ldots,x_{K,i} \right]\) is
the row }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{\(\boldsymbol{x_i} = \left[x_{i,1},x _{i,2}, \ldots,x_{i,K} \right]\) is
the }\DIFaddend vector of predictors of size \emph{K} for an individual seedling,
\(\phi_p\) is the random effect for seedling plots, \(\omega_t\) is the
random effect for different census, and \(\psi_m\) is the random effect
for the repeated observations of the same individuals (note that
\(\cdot\) denotes dot product). The set of predictor variables
(\(\boldsymbol{x_i}\)) includes intercept, log of seedling heights,
rainfall, densities of conspecific (\emph{ConS}) and heterospecific
(\emph{HetS}) seedlings, densities of conspecific (\emph{ConA}) and
heterospecific (\emph{HetA}) trees that are scaled by \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{0.24 }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{0.27 }\DIFaddend for the dry
season or \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{0.27 }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{0.24 }\DIFaddend for the rainy season, and the interactions of rains with
\emph{ConS}, with \emph{HetS}, with \emph{ConA} and with \emph{HetA}.

In the species-level regression, the \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{row }\DIFaddend vector of coefficients
(\(\boldsymbol{\beta_{1-K}}\)) of each species \emph{j} were assumed to
have a multivariate normal distribution through the Cholesky
factorization (\protect\hyperlink{ref-StanDevelopmentTeam2021}{Stan
Development Team 2021}),

\[
\boldsymbol{\beta_j} = \DIFdelbegin %DIFDELCMD < \boldsymbol{u_j} %%%
\DIFdel{\cdot }\DIFdelend \boldsymbol{\gamma_k} \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{\cdot }\boldsymbol{u_j} \DIFaddend + \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{(}\DIFdelend \mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma})\cdot \boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{z}\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{)^\top}\DIFdelend ,
\]

where
\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{\(\boldsymbol{u_{j}} = \left[u_{j,1}, u_{j,2}, \ldots, u_{j,L} \right]\)
is the }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{\(\boldsymbol{u_{j}} = \left[u_{1,j}, u_{2,j}, \ldots, u_{L,j} \right]\)
is the row }\DIFaddend vector of predictors of size \emph{L} for species \emph{j},
\emph{L} is the number of predictors for each species (i.e., the number
of traits including an intercept),
\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{\(\boldsymbol{\gamma_k} = \left[\gamma_{1,k}, \gamma_{2,k}, \ldots, \gamma_{L,k} \right]\)
is the the }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{\(\boldsymbol{\gamma_k} = \left[\gamma_{k,1}, \gamma_{k,2}, \ldots, \gamma_{k,L} \right]\)
is the }\DIFaddend coefficient \emph{L}-vector for \emph{k}th predictor in the
individual-level regression, \(\mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma})\) is
the diagonal matrix with the diagonal vector of coefficient scales,
\(\boldsymbol{L}\) is the Cholesky factor of the original correlation
matrix which can be derived using a Cholesky decomposition for the
covariance matrix of the original multivariate normal distribution,
\(\boldsymbol{z}\) is a \emph{K} \(\times\) \emph{J} matrix of latent
Gaussian variables, \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{and }\DIFaddend \emph{J} is the number of species\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, and \(\top\)
denotes the conjugate transpose}\DIFdelend . The set of
\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{species-level }\DIFaddend predictor variables (\(\boldsymbol{u_j}\)) includes LDMC,
SDMC, \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{RDMC, }\DIFdelend LA, SLA, Chl, LT, \(\delta\)C\textsubscript{13}, C, N, \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{CN, }\DIFdelend and
\(\pi\)\textsubscript{tlp}\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. The row vector \(\gamma_{k,1}\) represents
average effects of the each individual-level predictor (e.g.,
}\emph{\DIFadd{ConS}}\DIFadd{) across species, whereas \(\gamma_{k, l}\) (\(l \ne 1\)),
represents the effects of the }\emph{\DIFadd{l}}\DIFadd{-th individual-level predictor
(e.g., SLA) on the variation in the strength of the each
individual-level predictor (e.g., variation in the strength of
}\emph{\DIFadd{ConS}} \DIFadd{among species). }\DIFaddend To allow comparisons among parameter
estimates, \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{all the predictor
variables }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{the individual-level predictors }\DIFaddend (\(\boldsymbol{x_i}\)\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{and }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{) and
the species-level predictors (}\DIFaddend \(\boldsymbol{u_j}\)) were scaled to a
mean of 0 and standard deviation of \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{1.
}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{1 within each season and across
species, respectively.
}\DIFaddend 

Posterior distributions of all parameters were estimated using the
Hamiltonian Monte Carlo algorithm (HMC) implemented in Stan
(\protect\hyperlink{ref-Carpenter2017}{Carpenter \emph{et al.} 2017})
using the weakly-informative priors
(\protect\hyperlink{ref-Gelman2008}{Gelman \emph{et al.} 2008}).
Convergence of the posterior distribution was assessed with the
Gelman-Rubin statistic with a convergence threshold of 1.1 for all
parameters (\protect\hyperlink{ref-Gelman2013}{Gelman \emph{et al.}
2013}). \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{All statistical analyses were conducted in R version 4.2.1
(}\protect\hyperlink{ref-RCoreTeam2022}{R Core Team 2022}\DIFadd{) using the R
package }\emph{\DIFadd{targets}} \DIFadd{version 0.12.1 for workflow management
(}\protect\hyperlink{ref-Landau2021}{Landau 2021}\DIFadd{).
}\DIFaddend
